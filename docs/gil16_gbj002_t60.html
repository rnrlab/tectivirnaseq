GIL16 vs GBJ002 contrast

Author

Eduardo Lozano

Published

May 22, 2026

To analyze differential expression across strains and time points, we performed all statistical modeling within a single DESeq2 dataset rather than creating separate DESeq2 objects for each time point. Because the experimental design includes strong temporal dynamics associated with phage induction, library composition changes substantially across conditions. Analyzing all samples together allows DESeq2 to estimate size factors and dispersion parameters using the full dataset, thereby borrowing statistical strength across conditions and ensuring a consistent normalization framework. Importantly, replicate effects (treated as a batch factor in the model) are captured globally; splitting the dataset by time would force DESeq2 to re‑estimate normalization and dispersion independently for each subset, disrupting the shared technical structure of the experiment and potentially introducing artificial differences. Using a unified DESeq2 object therefore provides a coherent, statistically robust foundation that preserves biological comparability, controls technical variation and enables accurate strain‑level contrasts at each time point.

In order to perform the GIL16 vs GBJ002 contrast at 0 minutes, we established a log₂ fold-change (LFC) of ±0.585. This value reflects an increase or decrease in expression of 50%, depending on whether it is positive or negative, respectively. The default value is 0, but when performing the other contrasts we obtained too many differentially expressed (DE) genes. This violates one of DESeq2’s key assumptions, which is that most genes are not differentially expressed.

Code 
contrast <- results(dds, contrast = list(c("Strain_GIL16_vs_GBJ002", "Time60.StrainGIL16")), lfcThreshold = log2(1.5))
contrast_df <- as.data.frame(contrast)

contrast_df <- contrast_df %>%
  { x <- .; x$Gene_ID <- rownames(x); x } %>%
  left_join(
    annotation %>%
      filter(Gene_ID %in% rownames(contrast_df)) %>%
      dplyr::select(Gene_ID, Gene_symbol, Description, COG_category, Association, Type, Subtype, Source),
    by = "Gene_ID"
  ) %>%
  rename(
    Mean_normalized_counts = baseMean,
    LFC = log2FoldChange,
    Adjusted_p_value = padj
  ) %>%
  dplyr::select(
    Gene_ID,
    Gene_symbol,
    Description,
    COG_category,
    Association,
    Type,
    Subtype,
    Source,
    Mean_normalized_counts,
    LFC,
    Adjusted_p_value
  )

As can be seen in the summary() results, 493 upregulated and 512 downregulated genes were obtained. In addition, 97 genes were discarded due to low counts, likely because they are expressed in other contrasts. Below is an interactive table where all DE genes can be viewed individually.

MA plot

A MA plot visualizes the relationship between the mean expression and the LFC for each gene. It helps to identify genes that are differentially expressed across conditions, highlighting those with large fold changes relative to their expression level.

Code 
plotMA(contrast, alpha = 0.05, xlab = "Mean of normalized counts", ylab = "LFC")

DE genes

DE genes are defined as those with an adjusted p-value below 0.05. Among them, upregulated genes are extracted as those with a LFC > 0.58, while downregulated genes are defined as those with an LFC < -0.58.

Code 
up_genes <- rownames(subset(contrast, padj < 0.05 & log2FoldChange > log2(1.5) & !is.na(padj)))
down_genes <- rownames(subset(contrast, padj < 0.05 & log2FoldChange < -log2(1.5) & !is.na(padj)))

Volcano plot

A volcano plot displays each gene according to its LFC on the x-axis and the statistical significance (−log₁₀ adjusted p-value) on the y-axis. Among the upregulated genes, we find many chromosomal tRNA genes and two hypothetical proteins (GBJ002_05071 and GBJ002_05154) genes from pBtic235. Among the downregulated genes, we find genes mostly from the chromosome, but also seven from pBtic235, including GBJ002-DNT_253 with the lowest LFC and GBJ002-DNT_255.

Code 
volcano_plot(contrast_df, limits = c(-7, 7))

Enrichment analysis

In this section, we present the results of enrichment analyses based on the COG (Clusters of Orthologous Groups), KEGG (Kyoto Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes), GO (Gene Ontology) and EC (Enzyme Commission) categories for both upregulated and downregulated genes.

COG

According to COG categories, we found positive enrichment in inorganic ion transport and metabolism (P) due to 38 DE genes and negative enrichment in general function prediction only (R) due to 48 DE genes.

GO

The Gene Ontology (GO) is organized into three categories: Biological Process (BP), which describes the biological objectives to which gene products contribute; Molecular Function (MF), which refers to the elemental activities of gene products at the molecular level; and Cellular Component (CC), which specifies the locations within the cell where gene products are active.

  • According to BP, we find positive enrichments in processes related to translation, gene expression and nucleotide and amino acids metabolic processes. Negative enrichments were associated with regulation of RNA processes and DNA-templated transcription.

  • Regarding MF, we only observed enriched functions related to RNA binding and structural molecules functions.

  • Regarding CC, only positive enrichments were found, mainly related to ribosomes and protein complexes.

EC

The EC classification establishes up to four levels to define enzymes. In this section, the first three were analyzed, achieving significant results for all of them. At the first level, 1 and 7 numbers were positively enriched by 25 oxidorreductases and 14 translocases genes, respectively. In the second, 1.6 and 7.2 numbers were enriched by 6 and 5 upregulated genes, respectively. In the third, the same 6 genes positively enrich the 1.6.5 number, which correspond to NADH-quinone oxidoreductase subunits (GBJ002_02486, GBJ002_02489, GBJ002_02490, GBJ002_02491 and GBJ002_02492) genes and FMN-dependent NADH-azoreductase (GBJ002_04614) gene. Regarding the genes with 7.2 number, they are: calcium-translocating P-type ATPase SERCA-type (GBJ002_01084), magnesium-translocating P-type ATPase (GBJ002_01361), petrobactin import ATP-binding protein FpuC (GBJ002_01590), potassium-transporting ATPase subunit KdpA (GBJ002_03521) and potassium-transporting ATPase subunit KdpB (GBJ002_03522).
